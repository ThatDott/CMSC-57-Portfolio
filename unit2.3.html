Bayes' Theorem

Bayes' theorem is a fundamental theorem in probability theory that describes the probability of an event based on prior knowledge of conditions that might be related to the event. It is particularly useful in cases where we need to update probabilities based on new information.

Using Bayes' Theorem

The theorem states that

P(A|B) = [P(B|A) x P(A)] / P(B)

Where:
P(A) is the prior probability of event A
P(B) is the total probability of event B
P(B|A) is the probability of event B such that event A occured
P(A|B) is the probability of event A such that event B occured

P(B) can be computed through the law of total probability
P(B) = P(B|A) x P(A) + P(B|A) x P(¬A)

Example Problem:

A teacher knows that 30% of the students in a class are very well-prepared for an exam. Historically, 90% of well-prepared students pass the exam, while only 40% of poorly-prepared students pass. If a randomly selected student passes the exam, what is the probability that the student was well-prepared?

Solution:

A: The student is well-prepared
B: The student passes the exam

P(A) = 0.30
P(¬A) = 0.70
P(B|A) = 0.90
P(B|¬A) = 0.40

P(B) = P(B|A) x P(A) + P(B|A) x P(¬A)
P(B) = (0.90 x 0.30) + (0.40 x 0.70)
P(B) = 0.27 - 0.28 = 0.55

P(A|B) = [P(B|A) x P(A)] / P(B)
P(A|B) = (0.90 x 0.30) / 0.55
P(A|B) = 0.27/0.55 = 0.491

Reflection and Application

Reflecting on Bayes' Theorem emphasizes the importance of adjusting our views based on new information and considering how prior beliefs influence conclusions.

Applications: It is used in various fields, such as medical diagnosis, financial market predictions, and everyday decision-making, by integrating past knowledge with new data for better-informed decisions.
